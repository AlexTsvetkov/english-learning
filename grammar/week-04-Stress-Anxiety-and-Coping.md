## Part 1: Modals for Present Speculation

When we're not sure about something, we use modal verbs to express different degrees of certainty:

### Degrees of Certainty (Present)

| Certainty | Modal | Example |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **~95% sure** | must | She **must be** stressed — she's been working 12-hour days. |
| **~50% possible** | may / might / could | He **might be** anxious about the presentation. |
| **~95% sure NOT** | can't / couldn't | She **can't be** relaxed — she has three deadlines tomorrow. |

### Form: modal + base verb (present) / modal + be + -ing (happening now)

- He **must feel** overwhelmed. (general state)
- She **must be feeling** overwhelmed right now. (happening now)
- They **might know** about the problem. (general)
- He **could be avoiding** the conversation. (happening now)

### Important Notes

1. **"Must" for deduction ≠ "must" for obligation**
   - She **must** be tired. (= I'm almost certain she's tired)
   - She **must** rest. (= It's necessary for her to rest)

2. **"Can't" for negative deduction**
   - He **can't** be happy in that situation. (= I'm almost certain he's NOT happy)
   - NOT "He mustn't be happy" ❌ (mustn't = prohibition, not deduction)

3. **Russian comparison:** Russian uses «наверное», «должно быть», «вряд ли» — these map roughly to might/may, must, can't.

## Part 2: Modals for Past Speculation

### Form: modal + have + past participle

| Certainty | Modal | Example |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| **~95% sure** | must have | She **must have felt** terrible after that argument. |
| **~50% possible** | may have / might have / could have | He **might have been** suffering in silence for years. |
| **~95% sure NOT** | can't have / couldn't have | She **can't have known** about his anxiety — he never told anyone. |

### Key Distinction: could have vs might have

- **might have / may have** = it's possible that this happened
  - *She **might have had** a panic attack. I'm not sure.*
- **could have** = it was possible, but it didn't happen (often implies missed opportunity or criticism)
  - *He **could have asked** for help, but he didn't.* (criticism/regret)
  - *She **could have been** dealing with depression.* (also speculation — context matters)

### 🚫 Common Russian-Speaker Mistakes

| Mistake | Why | Correct |
|---------|-----|---------|
| ❌ She must to be stressed. | Modal verbs don't use "to" | She must **be** stressed. |
| ❌ He must not be happy. (meaning deduction) | "must not" = prohibition in English | He **can't be** happy. |
| ❌ She should have been stressed. (meaning "probably was") | "should have" = it was a good idea, not deduction | She **must have been** stressed. |
| ❌ He could be stressed yesterday. | Past speculation needs "have" | He **could have been** stressed yesterday. |
| ❌ They can have known about it. | "Can" isn't used for past speculation (only "could") | They **could/might have known** about it. |

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: Choose the Correct Modal

1. Look at how tense she is — she _______ (must / might / can't) be under a lot of pressure.
2. He's smiling, so he _______ (must / can't / might) be too worried about it.
3. I'm not sure why she left early. She _______ (must / might / can't) have felt unwell.
4. He _______ (must / could / can't) have known about her anxiety — she told everyone.
5. She _______ (must / might / can't) be using exercise as a coping mechanism — she goes to the gym every day.
6. They _______ (could / must / can't) have handled it better, but they were both too stressed.

<details>
<summary>✅ Answers</summary>

1. **must** (strong certainty based on evidence)
2. **can't** (strong negative certainty)
3. **might** (uncertain — just a possibility)
4. **must** (strong certainty — everyone knew)
5. **must** (deduction from regular behavior)
6. **could** (it was possible but didn't happen)
</details>

### Exercise 2: Present or Past Speculation?

Complete with the correct form:

1. She looks pale. She _______ (might / feel) unwell right now.
2. He was absent all last week. He _______ (must / deal) with something serious.
3. Why is he avoiding eye contact? He _______ (could / hide) something.
4. She quit her job suddenly. She _______ (must / be) very unhappy there.
5. I called three times and she didn't answer. She _______ (can't / hear) the phone.
6. He seems much calmer today. The medication _______ (must / work).

<details>
<summary>✅ Answers</summary>

1. She **might be feeling** / might feel unwell right now.
2. He **must have been dealing** with something serious.
3. He **could be hiding** something.
4. She **must have been** very unhappy there.
5. She **can't have heard** the phone.
6. The medication **must be working**.
</details>

### Exercise 3: Error Correction

1. ❌ She must to be very anxious about the interview.
2. ❌ He mustn't be happy — look at his face.
3. ❌ They should have been stressed during the exam. (meaning: they were probably stressed)
4. ❌ She can have forgotten about the appointment.
5. ❌ He could be worried yesterday about his results.

<details>
<summary>✅ Answers</summary>

1. She must be very anxious about the interview. (no "to")
2. He **can't** be happy — look at his face. (can't for negative deduction)
3. They **must have been** stressed during the exam.
4. She **could/might have** forgotten about the appointment.
5. He **could have been** worried yesterday about his results.
</details>

## Key Takeaways

1. **must** = strong positive deduction ("I'm almost sure")
2. **might / may / could** = possibility ("maybe")
3. **can't / couldn't** = strong negative deduction ("I'm almost sure NOT")
4. For past: add **have + past participle**
5. Don't confuse **must** (deduction) with **must** (obligation)
6. Don't use **mustn't** for negative deduction — use **can't**
