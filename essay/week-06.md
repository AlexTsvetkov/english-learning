## Model Text

### *Learning to Live With What Happened*

I was nineteen when I first realized something was wrong. I was sitting in a university lecture, listening to a professor discuss stress responses, when suddenly my heart began to race. The room seemed to shrink. I couldn't breathe. I gripped the edge of my desk and told myself it would pass. It didn't.

That was my first panic attack, though I didn't know it at the time. What I did know was that something I had been suppressing for years — something I had never spoken about — was pushing its way to the surface. It is widely acknowledged that unprocessed trauma doesn't disappear; it waits. And mine had waited long enough.

The months that followed were some of the hardest of my life. I was diagnosed with PTSD, having experienced emotional neglect throughout my childhood. I learned that the emotional numbness I had always considered "being strong" was actually a form of dissociation. My therapist helped me understand that healing doesn't mean forgetting — it means learning to carry the weight without being crushed by it.

Recovery, I discovered, is not a straight line. There were days when I felt I had made a breakthrough, only to be pulled back by a flashback or a wave of shame. But gradually, something shifted. I began to develop what psychologists call "post-traumatic growth" — a deeper understanding of myself and greater compassion for others. Having worked through my pain, I found that it had left me not weaker, but more resilient.

Today, I can say that what happened to me was terrible, but it does not define me. I am more than my trauma. I am a survivor.

*(271 words)*

## Model Text Analysis

### Narrative Techniques Used

| Technique | Example from Text | Effect |
|-----------|------------------|--------|
| **Vivid scene-setting** | "I was sitting in a university lecture..." | Draws the reader in, creates immediacy |
| **Sensory details** | "my heart began to race. The room seemed to shrink." | Makes the experience feel real |
| **Short dramatic sentence** | "It didn't." | Creates impact through brevity |
| **Personal reflection** | "something I had been suppressing for years" | Shows inner world of the narrator |
| **Time markers** | "I was nineteen...", "The months that followed...", "Today..." | Creates narrative structure |
| **Metaphor** | "learning to carry the weight without being crushed" | Makes abstract ideas concrete |
| **Full-circle ending** | Opens with fear → ends with strength | Creates satisfying closure |

### Grammar: Passive Voice
- "It **is widely acknowledged** that..."
- "I **was diagnosed** with PTSD"
- "having **experienced** emotional neglect" (participle clause — preview of Week 10)

## Writing Technique: Using Narrative in Essays

### When to Use Stories in Essays

Narrative techniques make essays **personal, engaging, and memorable**. Use them:
- In **introductions** — as hooks
- In **body paragraphs** — as supporting examples
- In **conclusions** — to create emotional resonance

### Key Narrative Tools

#### 1. Scene-Setting
Put the reader INTO a moment:
- ❌ "I had a panic attack once."
- ✅ "I was sitting in a university lecture when my heart began to race and the room seemed to close in around me."

#### 2. Sensory Details
Use sight, sound, touch, smell, and emotion:
- *I could hear my own heartbeat. My palms were sweating. The fluorescent lights felt unbearably bright.*

#### 3. Show, Don't Tell
- ❌ "I was scared." (telling)
- ✅ "My hands trembled. I pressed my back against the wall." (showing)

#### 4. Short Sentences for Impact
- *I told myself it would pass. It didn't.*
- *She said she was fine. She wasn't.*

#### 5. Time Markers for Structure
- *At first... / In the beginning...*
- *Over the following weeks...*
- *Gradually... / Eventually...*
- *Looking back now... / Today...*

#### 6. Reflection — Connect Story to Argument
Always explain WHY you told this story:
- *This experience taught me that...*
- *Looking back, I realize that...*
- *It was through this experience that I understood...*

### Balance: Story + Analysis

In an academic essay, don't ONLY tell a story. Follow this pattern:

```
STORY (2-3 sentences) → REFLECTION (1 sentence) → GENERAL POINT (1-2 sentences)
```

**Example:**
*When I was nineteen, I had my first panic attack in the middle of a lecture. [STORY] Looking back, I now understand that it was years of suppressed trauma finally surfacing. [REFLECTION] It is widely known that unprocessed trauma manifests physically, often at unexpected moments. [GENERAL POINT]*
