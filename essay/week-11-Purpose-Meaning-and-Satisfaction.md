## Model Text

### *What Makes a Life Worth Living?*

**Introduction:**
Viktor Frankl, a psychiatrist who survived the Holocaust, once observed that those who endured the concentration camps were not necessarily the physically strongest but those who had found a reason to keep living. His insight — that meaning is the most fundamental human need — remains as relevant today as it was in 1946. In an era of material abundance yet rising rates of depression and anxiety, the question "What makes a life worth living?" has never been more urgent. This essay argues that a life of purpose and contribution, rather than comfort and achievement, is the key to genuine fulfillment.

**Conclusion:**
When all is said and done, a meaningful life is not one free of suffering but one in which suffering has been transformed into wisdom. Purpose, as Frankl demonstrated, is not a luxury reserved for philosophers — it is a psychological necessity. In our relentless pursuit of happiness, we would do well to remember that happiness is not a destination but a byproduct of living with intention. The question, then, is not "How can I be happy?" but rather "What am I willing to dedicate my life to?" The answer to that question may well be the answer to everything.

## Analysis

### Introduction Techniques Used

| Element | Example | Purpose |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Hook (anecdote + authority)** | "Viktor Frankl... observed that..." | Grabs attention with a powerful story |
| **Context** | "In an era of material abundance yet rising depression..." | Shows why this matters NOW |
| **Thesis** | "a life of purpose and contribution... is the key" | Clear, arguable claim |

### Conclusion Techniques Used

| Element | Example | Purpose |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Summary (reworded)** | "a meaningful life is not one free of suffering..." | Restates thesis in new language |
| **Echo of introduction** | Returns to Frankl | Creates full-circle structure |
| **Broader implication** | "happiness is a byproduct" | Elevates beyond the specific topic |
| **Provocative final thought** | "What am I willing to dedicate my life to?" | Leaves reader thinking |

## Writing Technique: Introductions

### 5 Types of Hooks

| Type | Example | Best for |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Anecdote** | "When Viktor Frankl was asked..." | Personal/narrative essays |
| **Surprising fact** | "Studies show that lottery winners are no happier..." | Evidence-based essays |
| **Quote** | "As Nietzsche wrote, 'He who has a why...'" | Philosophical topics |
| **Rhetorical question** | "What would you do differently if you knew how little time you had?" | Persuasive essays |
| **Bold statement** | "Most people die without ever having truly lived." | Opinion essays |

### Introduction Formula

```
HOOK (1-2 sentences) — grab attention
↓
CONTEXT (1-2 sentences) — why does this matter?
↓
THESIS (1 sentence) — what will you argue?
```

### 🚫 Bad Introductions

| ❌ Avoid | Why |
|---------|-----|
| "In this essay, I will talk about..." | Boring, mechanical |
| "Since the beginning of time, humans have..." | Vague, overused |
| "The dictionary defines purpose as..." | Cliché |
| Starting with the thesis (no hook) | No engagement |

## Writing Technique: Conclusions

### What a Good Conclusion Does

1. **Restates the thesis** in different words (never copy-paste)
2. **Synthesizes** — shows how the arguments work together
3. **Broadens** — connects to a bigger idea
4. **Leaves an impression** — ends with something memorable

### 4 Ways to End an Essay

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Provocative question** | "If not now, when will you start living with purpose?" |
| **Call to action** | "It is time we stopped chasing happiness and started creating meaning." |
| **Full circle** | Return to the image/story from your introduction |
| **Quotation** | "As Frankl wrote, 'Life is never made unbearable by circumstances, but only by lack of meaning and purpose.'" |

### 🚫 Bad Conclusions

| ❌ Avoid | Why |
|---------|-----|
| "In conclusion, I discussed three points..." | Mechanical, adds nothing |
| Introducing completely new arguments | Conclusion should close, not open |
| "That's just my opinion though" | Undermines your essay |
| Ending on a weak or uncertain note | Leave the reader thinking, not confused |
