### *Why Naming Our Emotions Matters*

**Introduction:**
For most of my life, when someone asked me "How are you feeling?", I would answer "fine" or "not great." I had only a handful of words for the entire spectrum of human emotion. It was not until I started paying closer attention to my inner world that I realized how limited my emotional vocabulary was — and how much this limitation was holding me back.

**Body Paragraph 1:**
Psychologists have long argued that the ability to name our emotions — a skill called "emotional granularity" — is essential for mental health. Research by Lisa Feldman Barrett has shown that people who can distinguish between feeling "anxious" and feeling "irritable" tend to manage their emotions more effectively. When we label a feeling precisely, we reduce its intensity. This is sometimes called the "name it to tame it" principle: acknowledging an emotion takes away some of its power over us.

**Body Paragraph 2:**
However, many of us have never learned to identify our feelings with any precision. We might say we feel "bad" when in reality we feel resentful, overwhelmed, or melancholic — each of which requires a different response. I have experienced this myself: for years, I suppressed my emotions and called everything "stress." Only when I began to open up in therapy did I discover that what I had called "stress" was actually a mixture of grief, fear, and loneliness.

**Conclusion:**
Ultimately, naming our emotions is not a luxury — it is a fundamental skill for self-understanding and personal growth. We cannot address what we cannot identify. The journey toward emotional awareness begins with a single, honest question: *What am I actually feeling right now?*

*(287 words)*

## Model Text Analysis

### Structure
This essay follows the classic **four-paragraph structure**:

| Part | Purpose | What It Does Here |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| **Introduction** | Hook + Background + Thesis | Personal anecdote → problem statement → thesis |
| **Body 1** | Supporting argument with evidence | Scientific research supporting the main idea |
| **Body 2** | Personal experience / counterpoint | Personal example + transition from problem to solution |
| **Conclusion** | Restate thesis + final thought | Restates importance + ends with a provocative question |

### Grammar Highlights
- **Present Perfect:** "Psychologists **have** long **argued**...", "I **have experienced** this myself"
- **Past Simple:** "I **realized**...", "I **began** to open up..."
- **Stative verbs:** "We **cannot address** what we **cannot identify**"
- **Passive voice (preview):** "This is sometimes **called** the 'name it to tame it' principle"

### Vocabulary from This Week
- emotional granularity, anxious, irritable, resentful, overwhelmed, melancholic
- suppress, acknowledge, identify, open up
- "name it to tame it" (fixed expression from psychology)

### Cohesion Devices
- **Linking words:** "It was not until...", "However", "Ultimately"
- **Reference words:** "this limitation", "each of which", "this myself"
- **Contrast:** "We might say... when in reality..."

## Writing Technique: Essay Structure & Thesis Statements

### The Four-Paragraph Essay

For B2-level writing, master this basic structure first:

```
1. INTRODUCTION (3–5 sentences)
   → Hook (grab attention)
   → Background (context)
   → Thesis statement (your main argument)

2. BODY PARAGRAPH 1 (5–7 sentences)
   → Topic sentence (main idea of this paragraph)
   → Supporting details (evidence, examples, research)
   → Concluding sentence (link to thesis)

3. BODY PARAGRAPH 2 (5–7 sentences)
   → Topic sentence
   → Supporting details (different angle, personal example, counterargument)
   → Concluding sentence

4. CONCLUSION (3–4 sentences)
   → Restate thesis (in different words)
   → Final thought (question, call to action, or broader implication)
```

### Writing a Strong Thesis Statement

A **thesis statement** is the ONE sentence that tells the reader what your essay argues. It should be:

- **Specific** (not vague)
- **Arguable** (not just a fact)
- **Clear** (the reader knows exactly what to expect)

| ❌ Weak Thesis | ✅ Strong Thesis |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Emotions are important. | Developing a precise vocabulary for our emotions is essential for effective self-regulation and personal growth. |
| Many people feel stressed. | The inability to distinguish between different negative emotions — such as anxiety, anger, and grief — prevents people from finding appropriate coping strategies. |
| Therapy can help people. | Therapy provides a structured environment where individuals can learn to identify, name, and process emotions they have long suppressed. |

### Thesis Statement Formula (for beginners)

> **[Topic]** is **[your position/argument]** because **[reason 1]** and **[reason 2]**.

Example: *Emotional literacy is crucial for mental well-being because it allows people to regulate their feelings more effectively and communicate their needs to others.*

### Types of Hooks

| Hook Type | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Personal anecdote** | "For most of my life, I couldn't name what I felt." |
| **Surprising fact/statistic** | "Studies show that the average person can name only three emotions: happy, sad, and angry." |
| **Quote** | "As psychologist Susan David writes, 'Emotions are data, not directives.'" |
| **Rhetorical question** | "When was the last time you stopped and asked yourself what you were truly feeling?" |
| **Bold statement** | "Most people are emotional illiterates — and they don't even know it." |

## Writing Task

### Your Essay Prompt

**"Why is it important to understand and name our emotions? How can emotional awareness improve a person's life?"**

Write a 250–350 word essay using the four-paragraph structure.

### Planning Template

**Step 1: Brainstorm (5 min)**
Write down all ideas that come to mind:
- Why is naming emotions important?
- What happens when people don't understand their emotions?
- What personal experience do I have with this?
- What have I read/heard about this topic?

**Step 2: Outline (5 min)**

```
INTRODUCTION:
- Hook: _______________
- Background: _______________
- Thesis: _______________

BODY 1:
- Topic sentence: _______________
- Supporting detail 1: _______________
- Supporting detail 2: _______________
- Concluding sentence: _______________

BODY 2:
- Topic sentence: _______________
- Supporting detail 1: _______________
- Supporting detail 2: _______________
- Concluding sentence: _______________

CONCLUSION:
- Restate thesis: _______________
- Final thought: _______________
```

**Step 3: Draft (20 min)**
Write your essay. Don't worry about perfection — just get your ideas down.

**Step 4: Revise (10 min)**
Check your essay against this criteria:

### Self-Evaluation Checklist

| Criterion | ✅ Check |
|-----------|---------|
| I have a clear introduction, two body paragraphs, and a conclusion | ☐ |
| My thesis statement is specific and arguable | ☐ |
| Each body paragraph starts with a topic sentence | ☐ |
| I used at least 5 vocabulary words from this week | ☐ |
| I used Present Perfect and Past Simple correctly | ☐ |
| I used linking words (however, ultimately, for instance) | ☐ |
| My conclusion restates my thesis in different words | ☐ |
| I checked for common Russian-speaker mistakes (articles, prepositions) | ☐ |
| My essay is 250–350 words | ☐ |
