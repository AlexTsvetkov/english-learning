# Final Project — Week 12

## Your Personal Growth Essay

### Prompt

**Write a 500–700 word essay titled: "My Journey Toward Understanding Myself Better"**

This essay should integrate grammar structures, vocabulary, and writing techniques from the entire course. It is both a writing exercise and a personal reflection on what you have learned — about English and about yourself.

---

## Requirements

### Content
- Reflect on your personal experience with emotions, self-understanding, or personal growth
- You may use real experiences or fictionalized ones
- Connect your personal story to broader psychological concepts you learned in this course

### Grammar (use at least 6 of these)
- [ ] Narrative tenses (Past Perfect Continuous, etc.)
- [ ] Reported speech with advanced reporting verbs
- [ ] Mixed or inverted conditional
- [ ] Modal perfect (must have, could have, etc.)
- [ ] Gerund/infinitive (advanced pattern)
- [ ] Impersonal passive ("It is believed that...")
- [ ] Relative clause (defining or reduced)
- [ ] Wish / If only
- [ ] Inversion for emphasis
- [ ] Participle clause
- [ ] Advanced discourse markers

### Vocabulary
- Use at least **15 words/phrases** from the course vocabulary (across different weeks)
- Use at least **3 phrasal verbs** from the course
- Use at least **2 idioms or fixed expressions**

### Writing Techniques
- [ ] Strong introduction with a hook
- [ ] Clear thesis statement
- [ ] Well-structured body paragraphs with topic sentences
- [ ] Cohesion (linking words, reference words, no repetition)
- [ ] Mix of opinion language and hedging
- [ ] At least one narrative/anecdotal passage
- [ ] Strong conclusion with a memorable ending

---

## Planning Template

### Step 1: Brainstorm (10 min)

Write freely about these questions:
- What was the most important thing I learned about myself?
- What emotion or pattern did I become more aware of?
- How has my understanding of psychology/mental health changed?
- What moment stands out as a turning point?
- What vocabulary from this course resonated most with me personally?

### Step 2: Choose Your Angle

Pick ONE central theme for your essay:
- Discovering an emotion you'd been suppressing
- Changing a destructive thought pattern
- Learning to set boundaries
- Overcoming anxiety or building resilience
- Finding purpose or meaning
- Accepting yourself (or a loss)

### Step 3: Outline

```
INTRODUCTION (60–80 words)
- Hook (anecdote, quote, or question): _______________
- Context: _______________
- Thesis: _______________

BODY 1 (120–150 words) — The "before": how things were
- Topic sentence: _______________
- Details / narrative: _______________
- Concluding sentence: _______________

BODY 2 (120–150 words) — The turning point / what changed
- Topic sentence: _______________
- Details / narrative: _______________
- Concluding sentence: _______________

BODY 3 (120–150 words) — The "after": what I understand now
- Topic sentence: _______________
- Connection to psychology concepts: _______________
- Concluding sentence: _______________

CONCLUSION (60–80 words)
- Restate thesis: _______________
- Broader reflection: _______________
- Memorable final thought: _______________
```

### Step 4: Draft (30–40 min)

Write your first draft. Don't aim for perfection — aim for completeness.

### Step 5: Self-Edit (15–20 min)

Use the self-editing process from Week 12's writing lesson:
1. Content check
2. Structure check
3. Grammar check (one category at a time)
4. Vocabulary & style check
5. Cohesion check
6. Read aloud

## Evaluation Rubric

| Criterion | Excellent (5) | Good (4) | Adequate (3) | Needs Work (2) |
|-----------|--------------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Content & Ideas** | Thoughtful, personal, connects to psychology | Good ideas, mostly personal | Generic ideas, little personal depth | Unfocused or superficial |
| **Grammar Accuracy** | Very few errors; complex structures used correctly | Minor errors; good use of complex structures | Some errors in complex structures | Frequent errors; avoids complex structures |
| **Vocabulary Range** | 15+ course words used naturally | 10-14 words; mostly natural | 5-9 words; some feel forced | Fewer than 5 course words |
| **Essay Structure** | Clear intro/body/conclusion; excellent cohesion | Good structure; adequate cohesion | Basic structure; weak transitions | No clear structure |
| **Writing Style** | Varied sentences; appropriate register; natural flow | Good variety; consistent register | Limited variety; occasional register issues | Repetitive; inconsistent register |

### Scoring
- **23-25:** Outstanding C1 performance
- **19-22:** Strong B2+/C1
- **15-18:** Solid B2 — review areas rated 3
- **Below 15:** Significant review needed — revisit relevant weeks

## After You Finish

1. **Celebrate.** You completed a 12-week course. That takes discipline and commitment.
2. **Re-read your Week 1 writing.** Compare it to your final project. Notice the difference.
3. **Keep a vocabulary notebook.** Review it weekly. The words you don't use, you lose.
4. **Keep writing.** Even 100 words a day in English will maintain your progress.
5. **Read in English.** Psychology books, articles, essays — in the topics you love.

### Recommended Reading (Psychology + English Practice)
- **Brené Brown** — *Daring Greatly* (vulnerability, shame, courage)
- **Daniel Goleman** — *Emotional Intelligence* (EQ, empathy)
- **Viktor Frankl** — *Man's Search for Meaning* (purpose, resilience)
- **Susan David** — *Emotional Agility* (emotions, flexibility)
- **Bessel van der Kolk** — *The Body Keeps the Score* (trauma, healing)
- **Carol Dweck** — *Mindset* (growth mindset)
