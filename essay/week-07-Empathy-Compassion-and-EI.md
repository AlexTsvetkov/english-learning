## Model Text (Formal)

### *The Role of Emotional Intelligence in Professional Settings*

It is widely acknowledged that emotional intelligence, often referred to as EQ, plays a crucial role in professional success. While traditional measures of intelligence focus on cognitive ability, research conducted by Daniel Goleman and others has demonstrated that the capacity to understand, manage, and effectively express emotions is equally — if not more — important in the workplace.

The significance of EQ in professional environments can be attributed to several factors. Firstly, individuals who possess high emotional intelligence are better equipped to build rapport with colleagues and clients. Their ability to engage in active listening, pick up on subtle emotional cues, and respond with empathy creates an atmosphere of trust and cooperation. Furthermore, self-regulation — a key component of EQ — enables professionals to manage conflict without resorting to impulsive or destructive behavior.

Moreover, leaders whose emotional intelligence is well-developed tend to inspire greater loyalty and productivity. This is because they demonstrate what Carl Rogers termed "unconditional positive regard" — a non-judgmental acceptance that makes others feel valued. In contrast, managers who lack empathy often create environments characterized by fear and disengagement.

In conclusion, the evidence strongly suggests that emotional intelligence is not merely a desirable trait but an essential professional competency. Organizations that invest in developing their employees' EQ are likely to see measurable improvements in teamwork, communication, and overall performance.

*(215 words)*

## Model Text (Informal — Same Topic)

### *Why EQ Matters More Than You Think at Work*

So here's the thing — you can be the smartest person in the room, but if you can't read people, you're going to struggle at work. That's basically what emotional intelligence is about: understanding your own feelings and picking up on other people's.

Think about the best boss you've ever had. Chances are, they weren't just competent — they actually listened. They noticed when you were having a bad day. They didn't freak out during a crisis. That's EQ in action.

On the flip side, we've all worked with someone who was brilliant but had zero people skills. They'd say the wrong thing at the wrong time, couldn't take feedback, and made everyone around them uncomfortable. All the brainpower in the world doesn't help if you can't connect with others.

The good news? EQ isn't something you're born with — it's something you can actually work on. Start by paying attention to how you react to stuff. Then try putting yourself in other people's shoes once in a while. It sounds simple, but honestly, it makes a huge difference.

*(178 words)*

## Comparative Analysis

| Feature | Formal | Informal |
|---------|--------|----------|
| **Vocabulary** | "attributed to", "possess", "competency" | "the thing is", "stuff", "zero" |
| **Sentence structure** | Long, complex sentences | Short, direct sentences |
| **Grammar** | Passive voice, relative clauses | Active voice, questions |
| **Contractions** | None (cannot, do not) | Many (can't, they'd, it's) |
| **Pronouns** | Third person (individuals, professionals) | First/second person (you, we, I) |
| **Tone** | Objective, academic | Conversational, personal |
| **Connectors** | Furthermore, Moreover, In contrast | So, basically, on the flip side |
| **Evidence** | Research references | Personal anecdotes |

## Writing Technique: Register and Tone

### What is Register?

Register is the level of formality in your writing. Choose based on:
- **Audience** (professor vs friend)
- **Purpose** (academic essay vs blog post)
- **Context** (exam vs social media)

### Formal Register — Academic Essays

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| Complex vocabulary | Slang, colloquialisms |
| Full forms (do not, cannot) | Contractions (don't, can't) |
| Impersonal constructions | "I think" at the start of every sentence |
| Passive voice where appropriate | Overly casual expressions |
| Hedging (tends to, appears to) | Absolute statements |

**Formal connectors:** Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Nevertheless, Consequently, In contrast

### Informal Register — Blogs, Journals, Casual Writing

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| Contractions | Overly stiff language |
| Personal pronouns (I, you, we) | Impersonal passive everywhere |
| Phrasal verbs (find out, pick up) | Excessive jargon |
| Questions to the reader | Pages of unbroken text |
| Short sentences for impact | Unnecessarily complex structures |

**Informal connectors:** So, Basically, Plus, The thing is, On the flip side

### Useful Formal ↔ Informal Equivalents

| Informal | Formal |
|----------|--------|
| kids | children |
| get better | improve |
| find out | discover, ascertain |
| deal with | address, manage |
| lots of | numerous, a significant number of |
| think about | consider, contemplate |
| good enough | adequate, sufficient |
| help | assist, facilitate |
| so / that's why | therefore, consequently |
| but | however, nevertheless |
