## Model Text

### *The Cost of Living Behind a Mask*

In a world that rewards conformity, being authentic requires extraordinary courage. Many people spend years — sometimes entire lifetimes — hiding behind carefully constructed personas, presenting to the world a version of themselves that feels acceptable rather than genuine. This essay argues that while maintaining a facade may seem protective, the long-term psychological cost of inauthenticity far outweighs any short-term comfort it provides.

One of the most significant consequences of inauthenticity is the erosion of self-esteem. When a person consistently suppresses their true feelings and opinions in order to fit in, they gradually internalize the message that who they really are is not good enough. This, in turn, creates a dependency on external validation — a need for others to confirm their worth. However, such validation is inherently fragile; it can be withdrawn at any moment. As a result, the individual finds themselves trapped in a cycle: the more they seek approval by conforming, the less they trust their own judgment, and the weaker their sense of self-worth becomes.

Furthermore, living behind a mask makes genuine connection nearly impossible. Intimacy — whether in friendships, romantic relationships, or therapeutic settings — requires vulnerability. Yet vulnerability is precisely what a facade is designed to prevent. Consequently, people who hide their authentic selves often report feeling isolated even in the company of others. They may have many acquaintances but few relationships that feel truly meaningful.

The path toward authenticity, while daunting, begins with self-acceptance. Only by acknowledging and embracing our imperfections can we begin to build an identity rooted in integrity rather than pretense. As Brené Brown has argued, "Authenticity is the daily practice of letting go of who we think we should be and embracing who we are." Not only does this lead to greater self-esteem, but it also opens the door to the genuine human connection we all crave.

*(289 words)*

## Model Text Analysis: Cohesion Devices

### Reference Words (pointing back to earlier ideas)

| Device | Example | What it refers to |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| **this** | "**This**, in turn, creates a dependency..." | The act of suppressing true feelings |
| **such** | "**Such** validation is inherently fragile" | External validation |
| **the individual** | "**The individual** finds themselves trapped" | The person discussed earlier |
| **they/themselves** | "the less **they** trust **their** own judgment" | The person hiding behind a mask |

### Connectors Between Sentences

| Connector | Function | Example from text |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| **in turn** | shows consequence/chain | "This, **in turn**, creates a dependency..." |
| **however** | contrast | "**However**, such validation is fragile..." |
| **as a result** | consequence | "**As a result**, the individual finds..." |
| **furthermore** | adds another major point | "**Furthermore**, living behind a mask..." |
| **yet** | contrast (formal) | "**Yet** vulnerability is precisely what..." |
| **consequently** | result | "**Consequently**, people who hide..." |
| **while** | concession | "**While** daunting, it begins with..." |
| **not only... but also** | addition + emphasis | "**Not only** does this lead to... **but** it also opens..." |

### Cohesion Between Paragraphs

| Transition | How it links |
|-----------|-------------|
| Para 1→2: "One of the most significant consequences..." | Introduces first consequence promised by thesis |
| Para 2→3: "Furthermore, living behind a mask..." | Adds second major argument |
| Para 3→4: "The path toward authenticity..." | Shifts from problem to solution |

## Writing Technique: Building Cohesion

### 1. Reference Words (Substitution & Pronoun Reference)

Instead of repeating the same word, use reference words:

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Pronoun** | "People suppress emotions. **They** often don't realize **they're** doing it." |
| **This/That + summary noun** | "She hid her feelings for years. **This avoidance** eventually caused burnout." |
| **Such** | "Many struggle with low self-esteem. **Such** insecurity often stems from childhood." |
| **The former / the latter** | "Both authenticity and conformity shape us. **The former** builds strength; **the latter** erodes it." |
| **Synonyms/paraphrasing** | "self-esteem" → "sense of self-worth" → "confidence in one's own value" |

### 2. Linking Within Paragraphs

| Function | Connectors |
|----------|-----------|
| **Adding** | moreover, furthermore, in addition, what is more, on top of that |
| **Contrasting** | however, nevertheless, yet, on the other hand, conversely |
| **Cause/Result** | therefore, consequently, as a result, thus, this leads to |
| **Exemplifying** | for instance, for example, such as, a case in point |
| **Specifying** | in particular, specifically, namely, that is to say |
| **Sequencing** | firstly, subsequently, finally, in the first place |

### 3. Linking Between Paragraphs

The **first sentence** of each new paragraph should connect to what came before:

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Transition word** | "**Furthermore**, living behind a mask..." |
| **Summary of previous point** | "**Having established that** facades damage self-esteem, let us now consider..." |
| **Contrast with previous** | "**While** the previous section focused on..., it is equally important to..." |
| **Building on previous** | "**This connection between** masking and isolation extends further..." |

### 🚫 Common Cohesion Mistakes

| ❌ | ✅ |
|----|-----|
| Repeating "self-esteem" 10 times | Use synonyms: self-worth, confidence, sense of value |
| Starting every sentence with "Also" | Vary: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, What is more |
| Using "but" for every contrast | Vary: However, Nevertheless, Yet, On the other hand |
| No clear link between paragraphs | Start each paragraph with a transition or reference to previous |
