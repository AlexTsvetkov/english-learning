## Model Text

### *How the Inner Critic Shapes Our Lives*

Many people are unaware that they carry a harsh, critical voice inside their heads — a voice that psychologists call the "inner critic." This internal narrator constantly judges our actions, predicts failure, and insists that we are not good enough. While some degree of self-evaluation is healthy, an overactive inner critic can lead to anxiety, low self-esteem, and even depression.

The inner critic often develops in childhood. When children are repeatedly criticized by parents, teachers, or peers, they internalize those messages and begin to repeat them to themselves. For instance, a child who is told "You're lazy" may grow up with a voice that says "You'll never achieve anything." Research in cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) has shown that these internalized beliefs become automatic thoughts — patterns so deeply embedded that we don't even notice them. Dr. Robert Firestone, who coined the term "critical inner voice," claimed that this voice is not our real point of view but a hostile, judgemental narrator that we mistakenly accept as truth.

The good news is that the inner critic can be challenged. CBT practitioners suggest that the first step is awareness: learning to notice when the inner critic speaks. The next step is to question its claims: "Is this thought based on evidence, or is it a cognitive distortion?" Finally, therapists encourage patients to reframe harsh self-talk into more compassionate, balanced statements. For example, instead of "I'm a failure," one might say: "I made a mistake, and that's a normal part of learning."

In conclusion, the inner critic is a powerful but ultimately unreliable narrator. By learning to recognize, challenge, and reframe its messages, we can begin to free ourselves from years of unnecessary self-punishment.

*(268 words)*

## Writing Technique: Paragraph Structure

### The Anatomy of a Perfect Paragraph

```
📌 TOPIC SENTENCE (1 sentence)
   What is this paragraph about?
   
📋 SUPPORTING DETAILS (3–5 sentences)
   Evidence, examples, explanations
   
🔚 CONCLUDING SENTENCE (1 sentence)
   Wrap up + connect to thesis / transition to next paragraph
```

### 1. Topic Sentence

The first sentence of a body paragraph tells the reader what this paragraph is about and how it connects to your thesis.

| ❌ Weak | ✅ Strong |
|---------|----------|
| "There are many things about the inner critic." | "The inner critic often develops in childhood through repeated negative messages." |
| "Self-talk is interesting." | "Negative self-talk creates a vicious circle that traps people in cycles of anxiety." |

**Tip for Russian speakers:** In Russian essays, the main idea often appears in the middle or end of a paragraph. In English academic writing, put it **first**.

### 2. Supporting Details

Use a mix of different types of support:

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Explanation** | "This happens because..." |
| **Evidence / Research** | "According to Dr. Barrett..." / "Studies show that..." |
| **Example** | "For instance, a child who is told..." |
| **Personal experience** | "In my own experience, I noticed that..." |
| **Expert quote** | "As psychologist Susan David writes, '...'" |

**Order matters!** Arrange details from general → specific, or from weakest → strongest argument.

### 3. Concluding Sentence

The last sentence should:
- Summarize the paragraph's main point, OR
- Transition smoothly to the next paragraph

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Summary** | "Clearly, childhood experiences play a significant role in shaping our inner dialogue." |
| **Transition** | "While understanding the origins of the inner critic is important, the question remains: can it be changed?" |

### Paragraph Cohesion

| Tool | Examples |
|------|---------|
| **Transition words** | Furthermore, Moreover, However, Nevertheless, As a result |
| **Reference words** | this, that, these, such, the former, the latter |
| **Key term repetition** | "inner critic... this internal voice... this harsh narrator" |
| **Pronoun reference** | "Children internalize messages. **They** begin to repeat **them**..." |
