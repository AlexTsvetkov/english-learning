# Week 11 — Reading: Purpose, Meaning, and Life Satisfaction

## Article: "The Second Mountain"

*A story about a man who had everything society promised would make him happy — and why it wasn't enough.*

---

At forty-four, David had everything. A corner office in a consulting firm. A house in the suburbs with a garden his wife maintained and a mortgage he barely noticed. Two healthy children, a reliable car, a pension plan, and a gym membership he used three times a week. His life was, by every conventional measure, a success.

He was also, by every internal measure, empty.

Not depressed — he had been assessed, and the therapist had said he didn't meet the criteria. Not anxious — his sleep was fine, his appetite normal, his relationships functional. Just... hollow. As if the life he had built was a beautifully furnished house with no one living in it.

He described the feeling to a friend over dinner: "I have everything I'm supposed to want. So why do I feel like I'm waiting for my real life to begin?"

His friend, who had recently gone through something similar, put down his fork and said: "You've climbed the first mountain. Now you need to find the second one."

### The First Mountain

The "first mountain" is a metaphor borrowed from the writer David Brooks, who describes the arc most of us follow: education, career, family, achievement. We climb because society tells us to. We pursue **aspirations** that were handed to us — good grades, good jobs, good salaries — and we measure our worth by how high we get.

David had climbed diligently. He had never questioned the route. From school to university to his first job to his promotion, each step felt like progress. He was **thriving** — or so he thought. His **well-being** was measured in output: revenue generated, projects completed, problems solved.

But somewhere around forty, the question changed. It was no longer "Am I successful?" but "Does this matter?" And the answer, which arrived not as an **epiphany** but as a slow, creeping unease, was: he didn't know.

This is what psychologists call an **existential** crisis — not a breakdown, but a reckoning. A confrontation with the fundamental questions that most of us spend our twenties and thirties avoiding: Why am I here? What is this for? What will I leave behind?

### Stagnation in Comfort

The paradox of David's situation was that comfort had become the enemy. He had achieved **contentment** — or what looked like it — but it had calcified into **complacency**. He wasn't growing. He wasn't struggling. He wasn't reaching for anything beyond the next quarterly review.

Erik Erikson, the developmental psychologist, described the central challenge of midlife as the tension between generativity and **stagnation**. Generativity means contributing to something beyond yourself — mentoring, creating, building for the future. **Stagnation** means turning inward, becoming self-absorbed, treading water.

David was treading water. His days had the rhythm of routine but not the pulse of **purpose**. He **lived for** weekends, endured Mondays, counted the hours until the children's bedtime. He had **settled for** a life that was comfortable but not meaningful — and he was beginning to suspect that the trade was a bad one.

"I'm not unhappy," he told his wife one evening. "I'm just not... anything."

She looked at him for a long time. "That," she said quietly, "might be worse."

### The Breakdown That Built Something

The catalyst came, as catalysts often do, uninvited. David's company restructured. His department was dissolved. His corner office, his title, his carefully constructed identity — gone, overnight.

He expected to feel devastated. Instead, he felt something closer to relief.

For twenty years, his **identity** had been inseparable from his job. He was "David, the senior consultant." Without the title, he didn't know who he was. The loss forced him to confront a question he'd been avoiding: if you strip away the career, the status, the achievements — what's left?

What followed was not a collapse but a **transformation**. Slowly, tentatively, David began to rebuild — not the career, but the self.

He started by asking a deceptively simple question: "What do I actually care about?"

Not "What am I good at?" or "What pays well?" or "What impresses people?" — but "What matters to me?" It was, he would later realize, the most important question he'd ever asked.

### Finding Purpose

Viktor Frankl, the psychiatrist who survived Auschwitz and wrote *Man's Search for Meaning*, argued that the primary human drive is not pleasure or power but **meaning**. People can endure extraordinary suffering if they believe it has **purpose**. Conversely, people can be surrounded by comfort and still feel hollow — because comfort without meaning is just luxury with an expiration date.

David began the slow work of **meaning-making** — the process of examining his experiences, values, and desires to construct a sense of **purpose**. He read. He journaled. He talked to people who seemed genuinely alive — not successful in the conventional sense, but radiant, engaged, present.

He noticed a pattern: the people who seemed most fulfilled were not the wealthiest or most accomplished. They were the ones who had found a **vocation** — work that felt less like a job and more like a **calling**. A teacher who lit up when describing a student's breakthrough. A social worker who, despite exhaustion, spoke about her clients with unmistakable love. A retired engineer who spent his mornings mentoring teenagers in math.

These people had something David lacked: they **lived for** something beyond themselves. Their **purpose** was not about personal advancement but about contribution.

### The Second Mountain

David's second mountain began with a volunteer position at a local community center, teaching financial literacy to adults who had never learned to manage money. It was unpaid, unglamorous, and profoundly humbling.

The first sessions were awkward. David was accustomed to boardrooms and slide decks, not community halls and folding chairs. But something shifted in the third week, when a woman named Fatima — a single mother of three who had emigrated from Syria — thanked him with tears in her eyes for explaining how a savings account worked.

"You helped me understand something I was too ashamed to ask about," she said.

David drove home that evening feeling something he hadn't felt in years. Not pride — he had felt plenty of pride in his career. Something deeper. Something warmer. **Fulfillment**.

He had discovered what the philosopher Frederick Buechner described as the place "where your deep gladness and the world's deep hunger meet." His **vocation** was not consulting. It was teaching. Not the subject — the act. The human connection of sharing knowledge with someone who needs it.

### Gratitude and the Bigger Picture

As David's life shifted, so did his perspective. He began to practice **gratitude** — not as a self-help exercise but as a genuine reorientation of attention. He noticed the morning light on the kitchen table. He appreciated his wife's laugh, which he had stopped hearing years ago. He felt thankful for the restructuring that had dismantled his old life — because without the destruction, the rebuilding would never have begun.

He also began to see **the bigger picture**. His career crisis, which had felt like a catastrophe, was actually a **paradigm shift** — a fundamental change in how he understood success. Success was not accumulation. It was alignment. Aligning your actions with your values. Aligning your time with your **purpose**. Aligning your energy with what actually matters.

This didn't mean abandoning ambition. David eventually found a new job — a smaller firm, lower salary, more meaningful work. He designed financial education programs for underserved communities. It was harder than consulting. It paid less. And it made him feel, for the first time in decades, that his life had weight.

### Looking Back, Moving Forward

**Looking back on** his journey now, David sees the crisis not as a failure but as a gift. A painful, disorienting, necessary gift.

"I spent twenty years climbing the wrong mountain," he says. "I was so focused on getting to the top that I never stopped to ask whether the view was worth it."

The **wisdom** he gained is simple but hard-won: **purpose** is not something you find once and carry forever. It evolves. It shifts. It requires regular attention and occasional reinvention. What gave your life meaning at twenty may not serve you at forty. The question "What matters?" must be asked again and again.

He also learned that **fulfillment** and comfort are not the same thing — and that choosing **fulfillment** sometimes means choosing discomfort. Leaving the safe path. Starting over. Admitting you were wrong.

"When all is said and done," David says, "happiness **boils down to** two things: connection and contribution. Everything else is decoration."

He pauses, then adds with a slight smile: "Though I do still like the gym membership."

Standing **at a crossroads** at forty-four, David chose the harder path. He chose **purpose** over prestige, **meaning** over material comfort, growth over **stagnation**. He chose to **flourish** rather than merely function.

And the view from the second mountain, he says, is incomparably better.

## 💬 Discussion Questions

1. David had a successful career but felt "hollow." Have you ever achieved something and found it didn't bring the satisfaction you expected? What happened?

2. The article distinguishes between **contentment** and **complacency**. Where is the line between being at peace with your life and becoming stagnant?

3. Viktor Frankl argued that **meaning** is the primary human drive. Do you agree? Is meaning more important than happiness?

4. David found his **vocation** through volunteering. Do you believe everyone has a **calling**, or is that a privilege available only to some people?

5. The concept of the "second mountain" suggests that midlife crises can lead to **transformation**. Do you think crises are necessary for growth, or can people change without them?

6. David says happiness **boils down to** "connection and contribution." Is this true for you? What would you add or change in that formula?

7. **Looking back on** your own life so far, what has been your most significant **paradigm shift** — a moment when your understanding of life fundamentally changed?
