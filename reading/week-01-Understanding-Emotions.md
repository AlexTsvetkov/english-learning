# Week 1 — Reading: Understanding Emotions

## Article: "The Day I Learned to Name My Feelings"

*A personal essay about emotional literacy and the power of naming what you feel.*

---

When I was twenty-six, I sat in a therapist's office for the first time and she asked me a simple question: "How are you feeling right now?" I opened my mouth, paused, and said, "I don't know. Bad, I guess."

She smiled gently. "Let's try to be more specific. Are you **anxious**? **Overwhelmed**? **Numb**?"

I stared at her. It had never occurred to me that there was a vocabulary for this — that "bad" was not a single feeling but could be broken down into dozens of distinct emotional states. That afternoon, something shifted inside me. I began my journey toward what psychologists call **emotional literacy** — the ability to **identify**, name, and understand emotions.

### The Problem with "Fine"

Most of us grow up learning to **suppress** our emotions rather than explore them. We're told to "toughen up" or to "keep a stiff upper lip." Boys, in particular, are taught to **bottle up** their feelings, while girls are often told they're "too emotional." By the time we reach adulthood, many of us have become **emotionally detached** — disconnected from our inner world without even realizing it.

Dr. Susan David, a psychologist at Harvard, argues that this emotional avoidance comes at a steep price. In her research, she found that people who habitually suppress their emotions are more likely to experience anxiety, depression, and even physical illness. The irony is painful: the more we try to ignore what we feel, the more **agitated** and **uneasy** we become.

### A Spectrum, Not a Switch

One of the most important lessons I learned in therapy was that emotions exist on a spectrum. I had always thought of feelings in binary terms: happy or sad, calm or angry. But the reality is far more nuanced.

Consider worry, for example. There's a vast difference between feeling mildly **nervous** before a presentation and being deeply **apprehensive** about the future of your career. Both are forms of worry, but they live in different places on the emotional spectrum. Similarly, sadness ranges from a quiet **melancholic** mood on a rainy afternoon to the crushing weight of grief that leaves you completely **overwhelmed**.

My therapist introduced me to the concept of the "emotion wheel" — a visual tool that maps dozens of feelings across categories. I discovered words I'd never used to describe my own experience. I learned that I often felt **ambivalent** — torn between contradictory desires — and that this was normal, not a sign of weakness. I realized that the heavy sensation in my chest after family dinners was **resentment**, not just "stress."

### The Power of Naming

Research in neuroscience supports the idea that naming an emotion reduces its intensity. When you **acknowledge** what you're feeling — even silently, to yourself — the brain's emotional center becomes less reactive. Psychologists call this "affect labeling." In simple terms: when you say "I feel **anxious**," the anxiety loosens its grip.

This was revolutionary for me. For years, I had experienced a kind of emotional fog — a vague heaviness that I couldn't explain. Once I started to **identify** specific feelings, the fog began to clear. I wasn't just "stressed" — I was **conflicted** about my career choices and **resentful** toward a friend who had hurt me months ago. Naming these feelings didn't make them disappear, but it made them manageable.

### Learning to Express

Identifying emotions was only the first step. The harder part was learning to **express** them. I had spent my whole life as someone who **held back** tears, who changed the subject when conversations became emotional, who smiled and said "I'm fine" when I was anything but.

My therapist encouraged me to **open up** — first to her, and then gradually to the people I trusted. The first time I told a close friend that I was feeling **vulnerable**, my voice shook. I expected her to dismiss me or pull away. Instead, she said, "Thank you for telling me. I feel that way too sometimes."

That moment felt like **a weight off my shoulders**. For the first time, I understood what it meant to be seen — not just the polished, **content** version of myself, but the messy, uncertain, sometimes **euphoric** and sometimes **numb** person underneath.

### Beyond Happiness

Our culture has an unhealthy obsession with happiness. Social media tells us we should be **fulfilled** at all times — grateful, productive, glowing. But real emotional health isn't about being permanently happy. It's about being honest.

Some days I feel **nostalgic** for a past that no longer exists. Some days I feel **irritable** for no clear reason. And that's okay. The goal isn't to eliminate negative emotions — it's to make room for all of them. As my therapist once told me: "You don't have to like what you're feeling. You just have to let yourself feel it."

It took me years, but I eventually **came to terms with** the full range of my emotional life. I stopped trying to **bottle up** the difficult feelings and started treating them as information — signals from my body and mind that something needed attention.

And it all began with a single question: *"How are you feeling right now?"*
