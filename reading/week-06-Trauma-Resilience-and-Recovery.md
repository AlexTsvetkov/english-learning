# Week 6 — Reading: Trauma, Resilience, and Recovery

## Article: "The Body Remembers"

*A psychologist's perspective on how trauma lives in the body — and how we can begin to heal.*

---

The first time I met Katya, she told me she was fine. She sat in my office with her back straight, her hands folded in her lap, and a perfectly composed expression. "I don't know why I'm here," she said. "Everything happened a long time ago. I should be over it by now."

I've been a trauma therapist for fifteen years. If there's one sentence I hear more than any other, it's this: "I should be able to just **get over** it." The implication is always the same — that **trauma** has an expiration date, that pain is supposed to dissolve on schedule, and that the inability to move on is a personal failing.

It isn't. **Trauma** doesn't work that way. And the sooner we understand this, the sooner real **healing** can begin.

### What Trauma Actually Is

Most people think of trauma as a specific event: a car accident, a natural disaster, an act of violence. And it can be. But trauma is far broader than that. It includes emotional abuse, chronic **neglect**, growing up in an environment of constant fear, losing someone without the chance to say goodbye. **Trauma** is not defined by what happened to you — it's defined by how the experience affected your nervous system.

When something overwhelming happens — something that exceeds our capacity to cope — the brain's alarm system activates. Stress hormones flood the body. The thinking brain goes offline, and the survival brain takes over: fight, flight, or freeze. This response is brilliantly designed for short-term emergencies. The problem is that for many **survivors**, the emergency never quite ends.

Katya had experienced emotional abuse from a stepfather throughout her childhood. Nothing physically dramatic — no bruises, no hospital visits. Just years of constant criticism, unpredictability, and **shame**. By the time she left home at eighteen, she had internalized a message so deep it felt like fact: *I am broken. I deserve what happened to me.*

She had carried this belief for twenty years.

### The Invisible Symptoms

Katya was a successful architect. She was intelligent, articulate, and deeply private. Nobody at work suspected anything. But beneath the surface, the symptoms were relentless.

She experienced **hypervigilance** — a constant state of alertness, as if danger could appear at any moment. In restaurants, she always sat with her back to the wall. She flinched at loud voices. She could never fully relax.

She also suffered from **flashbacks** — not the cinematic kind, with vivid images and sounds, but what therapists call "emotional flashbacks." Without warning, she would be flooded with the feelings of her childhood: terror, **helplessness**, **shame**. The trigger might be something minor — a tone of voice, a closed door, a moment of silence that lasted too long. Her rational mind knew she was safe. Her body didn't.

And then there was the **numbness**. Katya described it as "watching my life through glass." She could function — work, cook, socialize — but she couldn't feel. Joy, sadness, anger — everything was muted, as if someone had turned down the volume on her emotional life. This **dissociation** was her mind's oldest defense mechanism: if you can't escape the pain, you can at least stop feeling it.

### "You Don't Just Get Over It"

One of the most harmful myths about trauma is that time heals all **wounds**. It doesn't. Time, by itself, does very little. What heals is **processing** — the deliberate, supported work of revisiting painful experiences in a **safe space** and integrating them into your life story.

Without processing, trauma gets stored in the body. Bessel van der Kolk, the psychiatrist who wrote *The Body Keeps the Score*, describes it this way: traumatic memories aren't filed away neatly in the brain's archive. They remain fragmented, disorganized, and active — like open files on a computer that never got saved. The body continues to react as if the threat is ongoing, even decades later.

This is why Katya's shoulders tensed whenever someone raised their voice. This is why her heart raced in quiet rooms. Her body was still protecting her from a danger that no longer existed.

### The Road to Recovery

**Recovery** from trauma is not a straight line. It loops and spirals. There are good weeks and terrible weeks. There are breakthroughs followed by setbacks. The important thing is direction, not speed.

For Katya, the first step was simply acknowledging what had happened. For years, she had minimized her experience — "It wasn't that bad," "Other people had it worse," "He never actually hit me." In therapy, she slowly began to name her experience for what it was: abuse. She was not being dramatic. She was being accurate.

The next step was **working through** the emotions she had spent two decades avoiding. This is the hardest part of trauma therapy. It means sitting with **grief** — grief for the childhood she didn't have, for the safety she was denied, for the innocence that was stolen. It means feeling the **guilt** that many survivors carry — the irrational but powerful belief that somehow, it was their fault.

"I know it wasn't logical," Katya told me once, tears streaming down her face. "But I always thought if I had been better — quieter, smarter, more obedient — he would have been kinder."

This is the cruel mathematics of childhood trauma: the child blames themselves because the alternative — accepting that a caregiver is dangerous — is too terrifying to bear.

### EMDR and the Science of Healing

As part of her treatment, Katya tried **EMDR** — Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing. Developed in the late 1980s, EMDR uses bilateral stimulation (typically guided eye movements) to help the brain reprocess traumatic memories. The theory is that trauma gets "stuck" in the nervous system, and EMDR helps unstick it — moving the memory from its raw, fragmented state into a processed, integrated one.

Katya was skeptical at first. "You want me to follow your finger with my eyes and that will fix me?" But after several sessions, she noticed something remarkable. The memories didn't disappear, but they lost their charge. She could recall the events without the accompanying flood of terror. The past began to feel like the past — not a movie playing on repeat, but a chapter that had ended.

"It's not that I forgot," she said. "It's that I can finally remember without drowning."

### Post-Traumatic Growth

There's a concept in psychology called **post-traumatic growth** — the idea that struggling with trauma can lead not just to recovery, but to genuine positive transformation. People who experience post-traumatic growth often report a deeper appreciation for life, stronger relationships, and a clearer sense of what matters.

This is not the same as saying "**what doesn't kill you makes you stronger**." That phrase, while well-intentioned, oversimplifies a messy reality. Trauma destroys. It damages. It leaves scars that never fully fade. But for some people — with the right support, the right tools, and enough time — something unexpected grows in the ruins.

Katya didn't become a better person because of her trauma. She became a braver one. She learned to **break free** from the patterns that had controlled her life. She learned that asking for help was not weakness but courage. And she learned that **resilience** isn't the absence of pain — it's the willingness to keep going despite it.

### A Light at the End

The last time I saw Katya, she told me something I'll never forget. "For twenty years," she said, "I thought the story of my life had already been written. I thought I was damaged and that was the end of it. Now I know that the story is still being written — and I'm the one holding the pen."

She paused, then smiled — a real smile, reaching her eyes for what might have been the first time.

"I can see a **light at the end of the tunnel** now. It's still far away. But it's there."

Not every trauma story has a neat ending. Not every wound heals completely. But the work of **recovery** — slow, painful, courageous — is always, always worth beginning. Because on the other side of that tunnel is something that every **survivor** deserves: a life that belongs to them.

## 💬 Discussion Questions

1. Katya believed she should be able to just "**get over**" her trauma. Why do you think this belief is so common? Is it helpful or harmful?

2. The article describes emotional **flashbacks** — being flooded with childhood feelings without a clear visual memory. Have you heard of this before? How does it differ from what most people imagine flashbacks to be?

3. The author argues that time alone doesn't heal **wounds** — **processing** does. Do you agree? What's the difference between letting time pass and actively working through pain?

4. Katya carried **guilt** and **shame** for things that weren't her fault. Why do you think **survivors** so often blame themselves?

5. What do you think about **EMDR** as a therapy approach? Does the idea of reprocessing memories through eye movements seem credible to you?

6. The concept of **post-traumatic growth** suggests that some people grow stronger through suffering. Do you find this idea inspiring or potentially dismissive of people's pain?

7. The article ends with Katya saying she can see "a **light at the end of the tunnel**." What does hope look like in the context of **recovery** from trauma? Can you always find it?
