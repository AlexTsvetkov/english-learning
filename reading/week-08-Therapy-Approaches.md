# Week 8 — Reading: Therapy Approaches

## Article: "Three Rooms, Three Questions"

*A guide to three major therapy approaches — told through one woman's journey to find the right fit.*

---

When Lena decided to try therapy, she didn't realize she'd have to choose an approach. She thought therapy was therapy — you sit, you talk, you cry, you feel better. Instead, she found herself reading about acronyms (CBT? ACT? EMDR?), schools of thought (psychodynamic? humanistic? existential?), and endless lists of specializations. It was like trying to pick a restaurant in a foreign city without knowing the cuisine.

"I just want someone to help me stop feeling terrible," she told a friend.

"That's a great start," her friend said. "But what kind of terrible?"

It was, as it turned out, the right question.

### Room One: The Thought Detective (CBT)

Lena's first **therapist** practiced **CBT** — Cognitive Behavioral Therapy. Dr. Ivanov was warm but structured. Each **session** followed a pattern: they would review the week, identify problematic thoughts, examine the evidence, and practice alternatives. It was organized, logical, and — for Lena — surprisingly revelatory.

The core idea of **CBT** is deceptively simple: your feelings are shaped by your thoughts, and your thoughts are often inaccurate. The goal is to catch these distortions in real time and replace them with more balanced interpretations.

In her first session, Dr. Ivanov asked Lena to describe a recent moment when she felt overwhelmed. She told him about a work presentation that had gone poorly.

"What went through your mind immediately after?" he asked.

"That I'm incompetent. That everyone in the room was judging me. That I'll never succeed at this job."

Dr. Ivanov nodded. "These are called **automatic thoughts** — they arise instantly, feel completely true, and are often wildly inaccurate. Let's examine the evidence."

Over the next few weeks, Lena kept a **thought record** — a written log where she tracked situations, the thoughts they triggered, the emotions she felt, and the evidence for and against each thought. She was astonished by what she found. The thought "everyone was judging me" had no evidence supporting it — two colleagues had actually complimented her afterward. The thought "I'll never succeed" ignored three years of strong performance reviews.

"Your brain is giving you data," Dr. Ivanov explained, "but the data is filtered through a biased lens. **CBT** teaches you to clean the lens."

The most powerful technique was what Dr. Ivanov called a **behavioral experiment**. Lena believed that if she spoke up in meetings, people would think she was foolish. So they designed an experiment: speak up once in the next meeting and observe what actually happened. The result? Nobody laughed. Two people agreed with her point. One person said, "Good question."

The belief collapsed like a house of cards.

Lena also tried **exposure** — gradually facing situations she had been avoiding. She had been dodging her boss's one-on-one meetings for weeks, convinced they would be catastrophic. With Dr. Ivanov's support, she attended one. Then another. Each time, the feared outcome failed to materialize, and the anxiety diminished.

After four months, Lena had **come a long way**. Her anxiety had decreased significantly. But something nagged at her. CBT had taught her to think differently — but she still didn't understand *why* she thought that way in the first place.

### Room Two: The Archaeologist (Psychodynamic Therapy)

Lena's second therapist, Dr. Petrova, took a very different approach. There were no worksheets, no homework, no structured agendas. Instead, Dr. Petrova asked open-ended questions and let the conversation go wherever it needed to go.

"Tell me about your mother," she said in the second session.

Lena laughed nervously. "Isn't that a cliché?"

"Clichés exist for a reason," Dr. Petrova replied with a gentle smile.

**Psychodynamic** therapy is rooted in the idea that much of our behavior is driven by **unconscious** processes — desires, fears, and conflicts that operate beneath our awareness. The therapist's job is to help the client **get to the root of** their patterns by exploring early relationships, recurring themes, and the hidden logic of the emotional life.

For Lena, the roots ran deep. Her mother had been loving but anxious — a woman who expressed care through worry. Every childhood accomplishment was met with "That's wonderful, but be careful." Every risk was greeted with a catalog of potential disasters. Lena had **internalized** a message: the world is dangerous, and success invites punishment.

Dr. Petrova introduced the concept of **transference** — the tendency to project feelings about important people in our lives onto the therapist. Lena noticed, with some discomfort, that she often sought Dr. Petrova's approval — just as she had sought her mother's. When Dr. Petrova was quiet, Lena felt anxious. When she smiled, Lena felt relieved. The therapy room had become a mirror of her oldest relationship.

"This is useful information," Dr. Petrova said. "It shows us the pattern. Now we can understand it — and eventually, change it."

The process was slow. There were no quick fixes, no cognitive shortcuts. But over months, Lena gained something CBT hadn't given her: **insight** — a deep, felt understanding of why she was the way she was. She began to see her anxiety not as a malfunction but as a perfectly logical response to the environment in which she'd grown up.

"I'm not broken," she said one day, with something like wonder. "I'm just... historically accurate."

Dr. Petrova laughed. It was **a turning point**.

### Room Three: The Compass (ACT)

By the time Lena found her third therapist, she was skeptical. CBT had given her tools. Psychodynamic therapy had given her understanding. But she still struggled with a persistent question: "What do I actually want from my life?"

Enter **ACT** — Acceptance and Commitment Therapy. If CBT asks "Is this thought true?" and psychodynamic therapy asks "Where does this thought come from?", ACT asks a different question entirely: "Is this thought useful? And what matters to you?"

**ACT** is built on two pillars. The first is **acceptance**: the willingness to experience difficult thoughts and feelings without fighting them. Not approving of them, not enjoying them — simply allowing them to exist. The second is commitment: taking action guided by your **values**, regardless of what your mind is telling you.

Lena's ACT therapist, Dr. Smirnov, began with a simple question: "What do your **values** tell you matters most?"

Lena paused. She had spent years managing her anxiety but had never asked herself what she actually wanted. After several sessions, she identified her core values: creativity, connection, and courage.

"Now," Dr. Smirnov said, "are you living in alignment with these values? Or is anxiety making the decisions for you?"

The answer was uncomfortable. Anxiety had been running the show for years. Lena avoided creative risks, kept relationships at a safe distance, and chose security over courage at every turn. She was safe — and miserable.

Dr. Smirnov introduced her to **defusion** — the practice of observing thoughts without believing them. "Your mind says 'You'll fail,'" he noted. "Can you notice that thought without obeying it? Can you say, 'I notice I'm having the thought that I'll fail,' and then do the thing anyway?"

This was different from CBT's approach of challenging the thought's accuracy. In ACT, you don't argue with the thought — you simply reduce its power. You **defuse** from it. The thought is still there, but you're no longer controlled by it.

**Acceptance**, too, was different from what Lena expected. It wasn't resignation. It wasn't giving up. It was more like putting down a rope in a tug-of-war. The monster on the other end of the rope — anxiety, self-doubt, fear — was still there. But you stopped pulling. You stopped fighting. And in doing so, you freed your hands for something better.

### Finding the Right Fit

Today, Lena doesn't see any single approach as "the answer." Each gave her something different. **CBT** taught her to question her thoughts. **Psychodynamic** therapy taught her to understand their origins. **ACT** taught her to live beyond them.

"It's like learning to drive," she told me. "CBT gave me the rules of the road. Psychodynamic therapy showed me why I was afraid of driving. And ACT told me where I actually wanted to go."

The most important thing, she says, was the **therapeutic** relationship itself — the **rapport** with each therapist, the trust that grew over time, the **confidentiality** that allowed her to say things she'd never said aloud. The specific techniques mattered, but the human connection mattered more.

"**Face your demons**," she says, quoting something she read somewhere. "But make sure you do it with someone who makes you feel safe."

She **opened up** slowly, across years and multiple approaches. She **worked on** herself with the patience of someone building a house brick by brick. And she discovered that therapy is not a single **intervention** — it's an ongoing conversation between who you are and who you want to become.

Lena has **come a long way**. She still feels anxious sometimes. She still hears her mother's voice whispering, "Be careful." But now she can smile at it, nod gently, and say: "Thank you. But I'm going anyway."

## 💬 Discussion Questions

1. Lena tried three different therapy approaches. If you were choosing therapy, which of the three — **CBT**, **psychodynamic**, or **ACT** — appeals to you most? Why?

2. **CBT** focuses on identifying and challenging **automatic thoughts**. Can you think of a recent situation where you had an automatic negative thought? Was it accurate?

3. **Psychodynamic** therapy explores how the past shapes the present. Do you believe that childhood experiences have a significant influence on adult behavior, or can people change regardless of their history?

4. **ACT** asks "Is this thought useful?" rather than "Is this thought true?" How does this shift in perspective change the way you might respond to anxiety?

5. The concept of **defusion** means observing your thoughts without believing them. Have you ever tried this? Is it easy or difficult for you to separate yourself from your thoughts?

6. Lena says the **therapeutic** relationship mattered more than any specific technique. Do you agree that the human connection with a **therapist** is more important than the approach they use?

7. The article ends with Lena acknowledging her mother's anxious voice but choosing to act anyway. What does it mean to **accept** difficult feelings without letting them control your behavior? Can you think of an example from your own life?
