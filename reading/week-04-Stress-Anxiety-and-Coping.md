# Week 4 — Reading: Stress, Anxiety, and Coping

## Article: "Running on Empty"

*A journalist's account of burnout — and the slow road back to balance.*

---

For three years, I wore my exhaustion like a badge of honor. I worked twelve-hour days, answered emails at midnight, and told anyone who would listen that I was "just really busy." I mistook **chronic stress** for dedication. I confused **fatigue** with productivity. And when my body finally gave out, I was genuinely surprised.

The collapse came on an ordinary Tuesday. I was sitting at my desk when my chest tightened, my vision blurred, and I couldn't breathe. My hands were shaking. I was convinced I was having a heart attack. A colleague called an ambulance. At the hospital, a calm doctor told me what had actually happened: a **panic attack**.

"Have you been under a lot of stress?" she asked.

I almost laughed. Stress was my entire life.

### The Slow Build

The thing about **burnout** is that it doesn't arrive suddenly. It creeps in, week by week, until the person you used to be is barely recognizable. Looking back, I can trace the warning signs across months.

First came the **insomnia**. I would lie awake until 2 or 3 AM, my mind racing through tomorrow's tasks. Then came the **irritability**. Small things that had never bothered me — a slow elevator, a loud colleague — made me want to scream. I was **on edge** constantly, as if my nervous system had been set to maximum alert and nobody had told it to stand down.

I started to **procrastinate** on important projects — not because I was lazy, but because the thought of starting them filled me with **dread**. The to-do list grew longer. The anxiety grew worse. I was trapped in a cycle where stress produced avoidance, and avoidance produced more stress.

My shoulders were permanently **tense**. I felt **restless** even when I had nothing to do — a buzzing, electric unease that made relaxation impossible. Some evenings I would come home, sit on the couch, and simply **shut down** — staring at the wall, unable to think or move or feel. My body had decided that if I wouldn't rest voluntarily, it would force me to stop.

### What Triggers What

In therapy — because yes, the panic attack finally drove me to therapy — I learned about **triggers**. A trigger is anything that sets off an emotional or physical stress response. For some people, it's a specific sound or smell. For others, it's a situation: public speaking, conflict, deadlines.

My triggers were subtle but relentless. The ping of a new email. The sight of my boss's name on my phone. The Sunday evening realization that Monday was coming. Each one sent a small jolt of adrenaline through my system. Individually, they were manageable. Collectively, over months and years, they **took a toll on** my health that I couldn't ignore.

My therapist explained it in terms I could understand: the human stress response evolved for short-term threats — a predator, a storm, a rival. It was never designed to run continuously. **Chronic stress** is like leaving your car engine running at full speed, day and night. Eventually, something breaks.

For me, the breaking point was that Tuesday afternoon. That panic attack was **the straw that broke the camel's back** — not a new problem, but the culmination of thousands of small ones.

### Learning to Cope

Recovery didn't come quickly. There was no single moment of healing. Instead, there were dozens of small, deliberate changes — new **coping mechanisms** to replace the old, broken ones.

The first thing my therapist taught me was **mindfulness** — the practice of paying attention to the present moment without judgment. I was skeptical. Sitting still and breathing seemed absurdly simple for a problem that felt so complex. But the research was compelling: regular mindfulness practice physically changes the brain, reducing activity in the amygdala (the brain's alarm system) and strengthening the prefrontal cortex (the part responsible for rational thought).

I started with five minutes a day. It was agonizing. My mind raced. My body itched. I wanted to check my phone. But gradually — over weeks, not days — something shifted. I began to notice the space between a **trigger** and my reaction. A stressful email would arrive, and instead of immediately **freaking out**, I could pause. Breathe. Choose how to respond.

My therapist also introduced me to **grounding** techniques — simple exercises designed to pull you back into your body when anxiety threatens to carry you away. The most basic one involves naming five things you can see, four you can touch, three you can hear, two you can smell, and one you can taste. It sounds trivial. During a panic attack, it's a lifeline.

I built a **self-care** routine for the first time in my adult life. Not the Instagram version — scented candles and face masks — but the real kind: sleeping eight hours, eating regular meals, moving my body, saying no to things I didn't have energy for. I learned to **wind down** in the evenings instead of working until I collapsed. I learned to **decompress** after difficult days instead of pushing through.

### The Nervous Wreck Who Learned to Rest

Six months after my breakdown, a friend asked me how I was doing. I said, "Better. Not perfect, but better."

"You seem calmer," she said. "More... present."

She was right. I was no longer **a nervous wreck** who **hyperventilated** at the thought of an empty inbox. I was still stressed sometimes — stress is part of life — but I had tools now. I had **coping mechanisms** that actually worked. I had learned to recognize when I was approaching my limit and to step back before I fell over it.

The biggest lesson was this: being **resilient** doesn't mean being unbreakable. It means knowing how to recover. It means building a life that doesn't require you to **burn out** in order to feel productive. It means accepting that rest is not laziness — it's maintenance.

I still feel **on edge** sometimes. I still **dread** certain conversations. But I no longer **lash out** at the people around me when I'm stressed. I no longer pretend I'm fine when I'm **at the end of my rope**. And I no longer believe that suffering is the price of success.

Some days, I even manage to sit in silence for twenty minutes and think about nothing at all. For a recovering workaholic, that's a miracle.
