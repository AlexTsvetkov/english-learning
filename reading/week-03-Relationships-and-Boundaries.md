# Week 3 — Reading: Relationships and Boundaries

## Article: "The Invisible Lines We Need to Draw"

*A story about growing up without boundaries — and learning to build them as an adult.*

---

When Elena was a child, her mother used to read her diary. Not secretly — openly, as if it were the most natural thing in the world. "I'm your mother," she would say. "I have a right to know what's going on in your head." Elena never protested. She didn't know she was allowed to.

Looking back, Elena can see that this was a form of **enmeshment** — an unhealthy closeness in which the **boundaries** between parent and child dissolve until it becomes impossible to tell where one person's feelings end and another's begin. At the time, though, it just felt normal. It felt like love.

### A Family Without Walls

Elena grew up in what therapists would call a **dysfunctional** family — not because anyone was cruel, but because emotional patterns were deeply unhealthy. Her father was emotionally absent, a quiet man who would **withdraw** into himself whenever tension arose. Her mother, starved of adult connection, turned to Elena for emotional support. By the age of twelve, Elena had become her mother's confidante, therapist, and best friend.

This role reversal is sometimes called "parentification," and it is a subtle form of emotional **neglect**. While Elena's physical needs were met — she was fed, clothed, taken to school — her emotional needs were systematically ignored. Nobody asked how *she* was feeling. Nobody noticed that she was **overwhelmed** by the weight of her mother's problems. Nobody taught her that it was okay to say no.

Instead, Elena learned to **accommodate**. She became the person who smoothed over every conflict, who said yes when she meant no, who bent over backwards to keep everyone happy. In psychological terms, she became a classic **people-pleaser** — someone who prioritizes others' emotions at the expense of their own.

### The Cost of Pleasing

By her mid-twenties, Elena's relationships followed a predictable pattern. She would meet someone, fall hard, and immediately begin to mold herself to fit their expectations. She was terrified of conflict. She would **put up with** behavior that made her miserable — cutting remarks, broken promises, one-sided effort — because the alternative felt worse. The alternative was being alone.

Her romantic relationships were almost always **codependent**. She clung to partners who needed fixing, pouring all her energy into their problems while ignoring her own. When a partner was moody or distant, she blamed herself. She would spend hours trying to figure out what she'd done wrong, **walking on eggshells** around their emotions.

One relationship was particularly **toxic**. Her partner used subtle **manipulation** — guilt trips, silent treatment, carefully worded comments designed to undermine her confidence. "I'm only saying this because I care about you," he would say, before delivering a devastating criticism. When Elena tried to raise concerns, he would **give her the cold shoulder** for days.

Elena didn't recognize it as manipulation at the time. She had no frame of reference for healthy relationships. She had never learned what **reciprocal** love looked like — a partnership where both people give and receive in equal measure, where effort flows in both directions.

### The Attachment Puzzle

In therapy, Elena learned about **attachment styles** — the patterns of relating to others that form in early childhood and shape our adult relationships. Her therapist explained that there are three main styles: **secure attachment**, **anxious attachment**, and **avoidant attachment**.

People with **secure attachment** feel comfortable with both closeness and independence. They trust their partners, communicate openly, and don't panic when conflict arises. This is what healthy relating looks like — and it's built on a foundation of consistent, responsive caregiving in childhood.

Elena, unsurprisingly, had developed an **anxious attachment** style. She craved closeness but was constantly afraid of abandonment. She became **clingy** when she sensed distance, needing constant reassurance that she was loved. Every unreturned text message felt like evidence of rejection. Every minor disagreement felt like the beginning of the end.

Her therapist pointed out something fascinating: Elena was consistently attracted to people with **avoidant attachment** — people who valued independence above all, who would **push away** anyone who got too close. The anxious partner pursues; the avoidant partner retreats. It's a painful dance that can continue for years, with neither person getting what they need.

"This is the pattern you learned in your family," the therapist said gently. "Your mother was enmeshed; your father was avoidant. You've been recreating this dynamic ever since."

### Learning to Draw the Line

The turning point came when Elena's therapist asked her a question that changed everything: "What would it look like if you **drew the line** somewhere? If you decided that certain behaviors were simply unacceptable?"

Elena stared at her blankly. She had never considered this. She had always believed that love meant enduring — that good people tolerated discomfort, that **standing up for yourself** was selfish.

But it isn't. Setting **boundaries** isn't selfish — it's essential. A boundary is simply a statement of what you will and won't accept. It's not an attack; it's information. "I need you to speak to me respectfully." "I'm not comfortable discussing this right now." "I care about you, but I can't be your only source of support."

Learning to be **assertive** was terrifying for Elena. The first time she told a friend, "I can't do that — it doesn't work for me," her heart was pounding. She fully expected the friendship to end. Instead, her friend said, "Okay, no problem. How about next week?"

Nothing collapsed. Nobody left. The world didn't end. And Elena realized something profound: the people who get upset when you set **boundaries** are usually the ones who were benefiting from you having none.

### Confronting the Past

The hardest boundary Elena ever set was with her mother. It took months of preparation in therapy. She rehearsed what she would say. She anticipated every possible reaction. And then, one Sunday afternoon, she sat across from her mother at the kitchen table and said:

"Mom, I love you. But I need to **confront** something that's been hurting me for a long time. When I was growing up, you treated me like your therapist. You told me things that were too much for a child. And I never felt like I was allowed to have my own feelings."

Her mother's face crumbled. There were tears, denials, and eventually — slowly — something resembling understanding. It wasn't a perfect conversation. Healing never is. But for Elena, it felt like **letting someone in** for the first time. Not by absorbing their pain, but by being honest about her own.

### Reciprocity and Repair

Today, Elena's relationships look different. She's learning to recognize the difference between **intimacy** and **enmeshment** — between genuine closeness and the blurring of identities. She understands that healthy love is **reciprocal**: it requires two people who are willing to give *and* receive, to speak *and* listen.

She still catches herself falling into old patterns. Sometimes she still **accommodates** when she should speak up. Sometimes she still feels the pull of **codependency** — the urge to fix someone else's problems instead of tending to her own. But now she notices. She pauses. She asks herself: "Is this what I want, or is this what I think I'm supposed to do?"

And when someone **oversteps** her boundaries — because people do, and they always will — she no longer crumbles. She takes a breath, and she says, calmly and clearly: "That doesn't work for me."

It's a small sentence. But for someone who spent thirty years without a single wall, it's everything.
